Your equipment
Tick off what you have. Don't buy anything new until your channel is live — work with what you own.
Smartphone or camera Must have
Your phone camera is enough to start — modern smartphones shoot in 1080p or 4K. Use what you have.
A way to keep your camera stable Must have
Tripod, phone stand, or prop it on a stack of books. Shaky video is distracting and easy to fix for free.
Audio solution Must have
Bad audio loses viewers faster than bad video. Built-in phone mic works in a quiet room. A $20 lapel mic is a game changer.
Lighting source Must have
A window with natural light facing you is completely free and often better than a ring light. Avoid overhead lighting — it casts shadows.
Script or outline nearby Must have
Print it out or have it open on a second screen just off camera. You don't need to memorize it — just know it well enough to glance.
Ring light or softbox Nice to have
A ring light ($25–$50) gives you consistent lighting year-round regardless of weather or time of day.
Lapel or USB microphone Nice to have
The single best first upgrade for most creators. A lapel mic clips to your shirt and dramatically improves audio quality for under $25.
External camera or webcam Upgrade later
Only invest in this once your channel is growing and earning. Your phone is genuinely enough for your first 20+ videos.

Remember: your first video's job is to exist — not to be perfect. Every creator you admire started with worse equipment than you have right now.

Your filming location
Run through this checklist before you press record. A good location makes everything easier.
Light source is in front of you, not behind
Face a window or your ring light. Never film with a bright window behind you — it turns you into a silhouette.
Room is as quiet as possible
Close windows, turn off fans and AC, silence your phone. Soft furnishings like rugs and curtains absorb echo naturally.
Background is clean and intentional
Tidy shelf, plain wall, or a simple backdrop. What is behind you says something about your brand — make it say something good.
Camera is at eye level or just above
Filming from below is unflattering and unprofessional. Stack books under your phone or tripod until the lens is level with your eyes.
You have enough room to move and breathe
Feeling cramped affects your energy on camera. Make sure you can sit or stand comfortably without feeling boxed in.
You can use this same spot consistently
Viewers recognize your setting. Consistency builds brand familiarity and speeds up your setup time every single shoot.
Before you press record
Run through these before every take — especially your first one. The prep takes 60 seconds and makes a real difference.
Script or outline is nearby and reviewed
You don't need to have it memorized — just read through it one more time so it feels familiar when you're on camera.
Phone notifications are silenced
Put your phone on Do Not Disturb. Nothing breaks a good take like a notification buzz mid-sentence.
You have taken three slow, deep breaths
This sounds simple because it is. It works. Nerves affect your voice and your pace — a breath before you start resets everything.
Posture is tall and relaxed
Sit forward in your chair or stand with your weight evenly placed. Slumping compresses your voice and reads as low energy on camera.
You have smiled before pressing record
Smiling before you start — even for a second — physically shifts your energy. Your viewer feels the difference even when they can't explain why.
You have committed to doing at least two takes
Give yourself permission to not be perfect on take one. Take two is almost always better. Plan for it from the start.
Test shot checklist

Before filming your real video, do a 60-second test recording and check each of these. Catching problems now saves you from re-filming later.

Audio is clear — no echo, buzz, or background noise
Play it back with headphones if you have them. Echo means you need softer furnishings or a smaller room.
Your face is well lit and easy to see
If your face looks dark or shadowed, move closer to your light source or reposition so the light faces you directly.
Framing looks good — head and shoulders centered
Leave a small amount of space above your head. You should fill roughly two-thirds of the frame — not too zoomed in or too far away.
Video is stable — no wobble or movement
If it's shaky, add more support under your tripod or phone stand. Even a rubber band around a stack of books can stabilize a phone.
Background looks clean and intentional on screen
What looks tidy in person can look cluttered on camera. Check your test recording and remove anything distracting from the background.
You are happy enough to film for real
It does not need to be perfect. It just needs to be good enough that you can work with it. If you can say yes — you are ready. Press record.
