Make It. Price It. Sell It.

Your complete guide to creating and selling digital products on YouTube

By Dee Dee Paradis  |  Confident Creator Circle

This guide walks you through every step we covered in the video -- plus answers to the questions most people have right after they watch. Save it, work through it, and come back when you're ready to launch.

1

How to Come Up With Your Product Idea

You don't need a new idea. You need to pay attention to the one that's already in front of you.

Start here: What do people ask you about all the time? Not what you think they should ask -- what they actually ask. In your comments, your DMs, your conversations. What's the thing you answer almost automatically because you've done it so many times?

That's your product.

Quick exercise: Write down the last 3 questions you were asked by a viewer, client, or follower. Look for the pattern. That pattern is your product idea.

  • If you're a coach -- what's the first thing clients always need before they can move forward?
  • If you're a course creator -- what do people ask before they even buy your course?
  • If you're a YouTube creator -- what have you figured out that you wish someone had handed you?
  • The more specific the problem, the better it sells. "How to write a YouTube description" will outsell "YouTube tips" every time.
2

What Makes a Digital Product Actually Sell

Not every idea turns into a product that sells. Here's the difference.

  • It solves a real, specific problem. Not a vague problem. Not a problem you invented. Something your viewer is experiencing right now and wants gone.
  • It's easy to consume. A 3-page checklist that someone actually uses beats a 40-page ebook they never open. Shorter and actionable wins.
  • It matches your content. The product should feel like a natural next step from the video. If they watched a video on YouTube descriptions, the product should go deeper on that exact thing.
  • It's priced correctly. See the next section for the full breakdown.

Free vs. Paid: Not every digital product needs a price tag. Free products (lead magnets) build your email list and earn trust. Paid products build your income. You need both working together.

3

How to Price It

Pricing is where most people either undercharge out of fear or overprice before they've built enough trust. Here's how the tiers actually work in the online digital product world.

$5–$19 Ultra Low

Where Most Creators Should Start

This is your testing ground. Low resistance, easy to grab, and works well from YouTube, Reels, and email. Start here to validate your idea, get your first buyers, and build confidence before you move up in price. You need volume to make real money at this tier, but the data you collect is worth it.

$20–$49 Low Ticket

For More Experienced Sellers or High-Impact Products

This range works best once you have some proof and trust built with your audience -- or when your product solves a high-intensity pain point and delivers a fast result. It still feels like an easy yes, but buyers expect more and you need to deliver it.

$50–$299 Mid Ticket

Mini Courses, Workshops, In-Depth Guides

These sell well but require a warmer audience -- someone who has watched you for a while and trusts you. You can absolutely sell mid ticket from YouTube, but it takes more runway to build that trust first.

$300+ High Ticket

Coaching, Done-For-You, Premium Programs

Requires a strong relationship, proven results, and a clear transformation. This isn't where you start -- it's where you go once your audience knows and trusts you deeply.

Pricing Tips That Actually Matter

  • Start in the $5–$19 range if you're just launching. Test your idea, get your first buyers, and collect proof before you move up.
  • Don't price by how long it took you to make it. Price by how much the problem costs your buyer to leave unsolved.
  • Round numbers feel arbitrary. $27, $37, $47 convert better than $25, $35, $45.
  • If it's not selling, price is rarely the problem. Usually it's the title, the description, or who's seeing it.
  • You can always raise your price as your audience grows and your trust builds. Start accessible, move up intentionally.

Two Ways to Create It

There's no one right way to build a digital product. Here are the two approaches I use -- pick the one that fits your time and your product.

Option 1

Canva + AI

Use AI to help you map out the structure and figure out the best order for your content first, then bring it into Canva to design. You write the copy -- AI just helps you get organized before you start.

✓ More design flexibility

✓ Polished, branded look

✓ Great for visual products

✗ Takes several hours

✗ More decisions to make

Option 2

Claude AI Artifact

Tell Claude what to build -- the topic, sections, what to cover. It creates a formatted, designed document. You review and refine.

✓ Significantly faster

✓ Sometimes under an hour

✓ Great for low ticket products

✗ Less custom design control

✗ May need refinement

Neither option is wrong. It comes down to how much time you have and what level of design your product needs. For most low ticket products, the Claude artifact approach is more than good enough.

How to Get Your Link and Set Up Checkout

Once your product is created, you need two things: a place to host the file and a checkout page. Here's how to keep it simple.

  • Upload your file to a platform like Stan, Kajabi, or Systeme.io. These handle the checkout and file delivery automatically.
  • Set your price on the platform. Someone pays, they get the file. That's it.
  • Copy your product link. This is the link you're going to put everywhere.
  • For free products -- connect to your email system so every download adds someone to your list automatically.

I use Stan. It's simple, clean, and takes minutes to set up. Upload your file, set your price, get your link. Done.

6

Three Places to Put Your Link on YouTube

Most creators put their link in one place and wonder why no one clicks it. Use all three of these -- every single time.

1

In the Video

Mention it on camera, naturally. Tell them what it is, why they need it, and where to get it. Keep it conversational -- not salesy.

2

Pinned Comment

Pin a comment with your link right after you publish. This is one of the most clicked spots on any video. Most creators skip it completely.

3

Above the Fold

Put your link in the first 2 lines of your description -- before "show more." If they have to click to see it, most won't.

7

What to Do If It Doesn't Sell Right Away

This happens to almost everyone and it doesn't mean your product is bad. It usually means something upstream needs adjusting. Here's what to check first.

🔍 Check your traffic first

How many people are actually seeing the video? If your views are low, not enough people have had the chance to buy. This isn't a product problem -- it's a visibility problem. Work on your title and thumbnail before you change anything about the product itself.

🎯 Check if the product matches the video

The product has to feel like the natural next step from what they just watched. If someone watches a video on on-camera confidence and you offer them a YouTube SEO guide, there's a mismatch. The tighter the connection between the video and the product, the higher the conversion.

💬 Check how you're talking about it

Are you actually telling people what it is, who it's for, and what problem it solves? A lot of creators mention their product almost apologetically -- too quickly, too vaguely, or buried at the end. Your CTA needs to be clear and confident. Say what it is. Say why they need it. Tell them where to get it.

💰 Check the price

If you're getting clicks but no purchases, the price might be the friction point. Try dropping it slightly and see if conversions change. If you're getting no clicks at all, price probably isn't the issue -- go back to visibility and messaging first.

⏳ Give it time

YouTube is a long game. A video that gets 50 views today might get 5,000 views six months from now when YouTube starts pushing it. Don't pull a product because it didn't sell in the first week. Put it out, keep creating, and let YouTube do its job.
