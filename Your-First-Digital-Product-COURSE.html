The One-Problem Rule

Module 1 · Lesson 1
Start small. Finish it.
The Rule Why Small Wins In Real Life Your Move
Core idea

Your first product is a first date, not a wedding.

Before you make a single thing, you need the right size in your head. This is where most people over 40 freeze. They picture some giant, polished program, the size of it scares them, and they go right back to doing nothing. So let's shrink it. Your first product has one job, and it's smaller than you think.

One problem. One outcome. Kept small.

Part one

One problem

Pick a single thing your viewer is stuck on. Not the whole journey, not five problems. One. The narrower it is, the easier it is to make and the easier it is to sell.

  • The thing people ask you about most
  • Something you can explain start to finish
  • A problem with a clear before and after
Part two

One outcome

They finish with one clear win they didn't have before they bought it. If you can't say the win in a single sentence, it's not focused enough yet.

  • Name the win out loud
  • One result, not a buffet
  • "By the end, you'll have ___"
Part three

Kept small

Small enough to make in an afternoon, cheap enough to be an easy yes. And small isn't lazy. It's focused. One problem solved really well beats ten solved halfway.

  • Made fast, not someday
  • Priced for the first yes
  • Focused, not flimsy
Why small wins

Going big feels impressive. It's also where people quit.

Here's what actually happens when you ignore the rule and reach for the big thing first.

The trap

The big-product trap

Someone finally decides to make money from their channel, so they sit down to build the giant signature course. Twelve modules. A workbook. The whole production.

  • Three weeks in, still not done
  • Exhausted and discouraged
  • Sold exactly nothing
  • Most people quit right here
vs
The win

The small-product win

You make one small, genuinely helpful thing, and you actually finish it this week. It's out in the world while the big-course folks are still on module two.

  • It's done, so it's real
  • Someone can buy it today
  • You get your first yes
  • Momentum, not burnout
In real life

Two easy ways to picture the right size.

You don't need a marketing degree to get this. You already know it from regular life.

Analogy one

The first date

You don't propose marriage on a first date. You grab a coffee. You let the other person decide if they like you before you ask for anything bigger.

Your first product is that coffee. Small, low pressure, and its whole job is to turn a stranger into someone who's said yes to you once.

Analogy two

The free sample

Think of the sample table at Costco. They don't hand you the whole rotisserie chicken and a bill. They give you one bite on a toothpick.

That bite isn't where they make their money. It's how they get you to trust the thing is good. Your first product is the bite, not the whole chicken.

“

The first yes is the hard one. Once someone's bought one small thing from you and you've actually helped them, every yes after that gets easier.

Before lesson 2

Make yourself one promise.

Small and finished beats big and perfect. Every single time. Hold onto that, because in the next lesson we're going to find the one problem your product is going to solve, and it's probably something you already know cold.

Find Your Idea

Module 1 · Lesson 2
You already know it.
Where to Look The Shortcut Gut Check Your Move
Core idea

You're not inventing an idea. You already have one.

This is where people overthink the hardest. They sit and wait for some brilliant, original idea to strike. It won't, and it doesn't need to. The best first product comes from something you already know cold and already help people with. Let's go dig it out.

Where to look

Your idea is hiding in plain sight.

Four places it's almost always sitting, waiting for you to notice it.

Source one

Your comments and DMs

The questions that show up again and again. People are literally telling you what they're stuck on. You just have to listen for the repeats.

  • The question you've answered ten times
  • The "how did you do that?" replies
  • The thing people thank you for
Source two

What you explain constantly

The thing you walk people through over and over, on camera and off. If you're a little tired of explaining it, that's a great sign. It means it's a product.

  • The mini-lecture you give friends
  • The steps you know by heart
  • The part others always find confusing
Source three

What tripped you up early

The wall you hit when you started, that you eventually figured out. You're now one step ahead of the person still stuck there, and that's all it takes.

  • The thing you wish you'd known
  • The mistake you'd save them from
  • Your "I finally cracked it" moment
Source four

What people already pay to fix

Look at what your audience already buys or hires help for. If they're paying someone else, they'll pay you for a simpler, clearer version.

  • The problem that's worth money to them
  • The shortcut they'd happily buy
  • The headache they want gone

If people keep asking you the same question, that question is your product.

The shortcut

Be the go-to friend

You know that one friend everybody calls about a certain thing? The go-to for tech help, or recipes, or finding a good deal? On your channel, you're that friend for something.

The thing people come to you for is the exact thing they'll pay you to package up and hand them in one place.

A relief

It doesn't have to be original

Your idea doesn't need to be brand new. It needs to be clear and helpful. A simpler explanation of something that already exists beats a clever idea nobody understands.

  • Clear beats clever
  • Helpful beats original
  • Done beats perfect
Gut check

Run your idea through three quick questions.

If you can answer yes to all three, you've found it. Stop looking and start building.

Test one

Can you name the win?

Finish this sentence: "By the end, they'll have ___." If you can fill that blank in one line, it's focused enough.

Test two

Can they use it fast?

One clear win they can get the same day, not a six-month transformation. Quick wins are what get bought and finished.

Test three

Do you actually know it?

You can teach this without Googling. You've done it, or you help people do it. That's your authority right there.

“

You don't need a new idea. You need a clear one. The thing you already explain for free is the thing people will gladly pay you to hand them in one place.

Before lesson 3

Write it in one sentence.

Fill in this blank before you move on: I help [who] [do what]. Get it down, even rough, even messy. That's your product idea. In the next lesson we'll turn it into the right kind of product, the format that fits both you and the problem.

Pick Your Format

Module 1 · Lesson 3
Pick one. Just one.
The Menu How to Choose One Format Your Move
Core idea

The format matters less than you think.

People stall here trying to pick the perfect format, like the wrong choice will sink the whole thing. It won't. A guide, a checklist, a template, a short workshop, any of them can be your first product. What matters is that it solves your one problem and that you'll actually finish it. Let's pick the one that fits you.

The menu

Four simple formats. Pick one.

Every one of these can be made fast and sold cheap. Here's what each is best at.

Format one

Guide

Walks them through the how, start to finish. Best when the win takes a few steps and people need the full path laid out.

Best forA process, a how-to, a start-to-finish method.

Format two

Checklist

The steps in order so nothing gets missed. Best when people kind of know what to do but forget pieces or get overwhelmed.

Best forA repeatable process, a setup, a launch.

Format three

Template

A fill-in-the-blanks shortcut they reuse. Best when you can hand them a head start instead of a blank page.

Best forScripts, planners, swipe files, layouts.

Format four

Mini workshop

A short recorded session that teaches one thing. Best when you teach better out loud than on paper and you're comfy on camera.

Best forA concept, a demo, a walk-through.

How to choose

Match it to the problem and your comfort.

Two questions decide it for you. That's the whole decision.

Question one

What does the problem need?

Some problems need the full walk-through. Some just need the steps, or a head start. Let the problem pick the shape.

  • Big messy problem → guide or workshop
  • Forgetting steps → checklist
  • Blank-page dread → template
Question two

What are you comfortable making?

Love writing? Guide or checklist. Happier talking than typing? Record a workshop. The best format is the one you'll actually finish.

  • Play to your strength
  • The one you'll finish wins
  • There's no wrong answer here
“

Picking a format is like picking a container for leftovers. Soup needs a tub, a sandwich needs a bag. You're not agonizing, you're just matching the container to what's going inside.

One format

One format. For now.

You can always add more later. Today, we keep it to one so it actually gets finished.

Why just one

One beats two half-built

One focused product is easier to make, easier to explain, and easier to buy. Two half-finished products help nobody and sell nothing.

  • Easier to finish
  • Easier to explain
  • Easier to buy
Later, not now

You can expand after proof

Once this one sells, you can turn a guide into a workshop or bundle a template alongside it. But that's a later problem. Today, just one.

  • First sale first
  • Expand after it works
  • Don't borrow tomorrow's work
Module 1 complete

Pick your format. Out loud.

Say it: my first product is a [format] that helps [who] [do what]. That's your whole first module done. You know the rule, you've got your idea, and now you've got your format. Next, we actually make the thing.

Map It Before You Make It

Module 2 · Lesson 1
Bones first.
Bones First The Map Cut It Down Your Move
Core idea

Outline it first. Then building is just filling in the blanks.

The reason a product takes forever isn't the making. It's sitting down to a blank page with no plan and trying to invent it as you go. So we don't do that. Before you make anything, you map it. Once the bones are down, the actual building is the easy part.

Bones first

Build the skeleton before the skin.

Every good product is just a clear path from where someone is now to the one win you promised. Get that path down first.

Step one

Start with the win

Write the ending first. "By the end they'll have ___." Everything else in the product exists only to get them there.

  • The promise is the destination
  • Work backward from it
  • If it doesn't serve the win, cut it
Step two

List the steps to get there

What are the steps someone takes to go from stuck to done? Just list them, in order. That list is your outline.

  • Brain-dump every step
  • Put them in order
  • That's your table of contents
Step three

One step, one section

Each step becomes one small section. Now you're not writing a whole product, you're just filling in a few small boxes.

  • Small sections, not chapters
  • One idea each
  • Easy to start, easy to finish
The map

Here's what a map looks like.

Say your product helps someone film their first video without freezing up. Once you write the promise and the steps, it almost builds itself.

★
The promise

By the end, you'll film your first video, start to finish.

This is the one win. Every step below points at it.

1

Pick one thing to talk about

Narrow it down so you're not staring at a blank brain.

2

Set up your phone and light

The three-minute version. No gear shopping required.

3

The one trick to stop freezing

What to do the second the nerves hit record.

4

Hit record and do one take

One take, all the way through. No restarting.

5

What "good enough" looks like

So they know when to stop and call video one done.

Cut it down

When in doubt, cut it out.

The first draft of any map has too much in it. Your job now is to remove everything that isn't the one outcome.

The trap

The "nice to know" pile

You'll want to add extra tips, bonus context, the whole history of the thing. Resist. Every extra adds time to build and confusion to use.

  • Cut the backstory
  • Cut the tangents
  • Cut the "while we're here..."
The keeper

Keep only what gets the win

If a section doesn't move them toward the one promised outcome, it doesn't belong in version one. Park it for later.

  • Serve the one outcome
  • Save the rest in a "later" list
  • Lean is a feature, not a flaw
“

A map you can build in a week beats a masterpiece you never finish. Get the bones down, cut it lean, and the making becomes almost boring. That's the goal.

Before lesson 2

Map yours in ten minutes.

Write your one-sentence promise at the top. List the steps to get there. Turn each step into a small section. Then cross out anything that isn't the win. That's your map. The next lesson is where we actually build it.

Build It

Module 2 · Lesson 2
Made, not perfect.
The Tool Make It Readable Export It Your Move
Core idea

You can build this in an afternoon, in a free tool.

Here's the part people dread and then can't believe how simple it is. You don't need design skills or fancy software. You need Canva, your map from the last lesson, and a couple of hours. Let's turn your outline into a real thing people can hold.

The tool

Canva does the hard part for you.

It's free, it runs in your browser, and it's built for people who don't think of themselves as designers. That's exactly who it's for.

Step one

Start from a template

You never start from a blank page. Search your format, pick a template that's close, then swap in your words. The layout's already done for you.

  • Search "ebook," "checklist," etc.
  • Pick one you like
  • Replace its text with yours
Step two

Drop your map in

Each section of your map becomes a page. You're pouring your outline in and dressing it up, not inventing anything from scratch.

  • One section per page
  • Headline, then your content
  • Follow the template's rhythm
Step three

Don't reinvent design

Use the template's fonts and spacing. Resist fiddling. The pros already made it look good. Your only job is clear words.

  • Trust the template
  • Words first, polish second
  • Clear beats clever
Make it readable

Readable beats fancy, every time.

A few simple rules keep it looking clean and professional with zero design background.

Rule one

Keep it clean

Lots of white space, text big enough to read easily, one idea per page. Crowded pages feel overwhelming and cheap. Roomy pages feel calm and worth paying for.

  • Big, readable text
  • Room to breathe
  • One idea per page
Rule two

Stay consistent

Same fonts, same colors, same spacing all the way through. Consistency is what makes homemade look professional. Pick a look and repeat it.

  • Two fonts, maximum
  • A small handful of colors
  • Repeat, don't reinvent
“

Think of it like setting a table. You don't need fancy china. A clean plate, a folded napkin, everything lined up, and it feels cared for. Tidy and consistent reads as "this person knows what they're doing."

Export it

Get it out of Canva and ready to send.

One last step turns your design into the file your buyer actually receives.

Do this

Export as a PDF

For a guide, checklist, or template, a PDF is your friend. It looks the same on every device and nobody can accidentally mess it up.

  • Download, then choose PDF
  • Name the file clearly
  • Open it to be sure it looks right
Then this

Give it one fresh read

Open the finished file and read it like a buyer would. Typos, broken spots, anything confusing. Fix those, and you're done.

  • Read it with fresh eyes
  • Fix the obvious stuff
  • Then stop touching it
Before lesson 3

Build it this week. Not someday.

Open Canva, pick a template, pour your map in, keep it clean and consistent, and export the PDF. Give yourself an afternoon, not a month. In the next lesson, we deal with the voice in your head saying it's not good enough yet.

Good Enough to Sell

Module 2 · Lesson 3
Done beats perfect.
Done Beats Perfect Focused, Not Lazy The Finish Line Your Move
Core idea

A finished "good" product beats a perfect one that doesn't exist.

This is where more first products die than anywhere else. Not in the making. In the endless polishing. You keep tweaking, second-guessing, adding one more thing, and it never goes live. Let's get you across the finish line.

Done beats perfect

Perfect is just hiding with better lighting.

Polishing forever feels productive. It's usually fear wearing a disguise. Here's what's really going on with your first product.

Reason one

Perfect doesn't sell

A perfect product in your head has helped zero people and earned zero dollars. A good one that's live can do both today.

  • Imaginary products help nobody
  • Live ones help and earn
  • Shipped beats flawless
Reason two

Selling it shows you what to fix

You learn what to improve from real buyers, not from more tweaking in the dark. Version one teaches you version two.

  • Real feedback beats guessing
  • You can update it later, easily
  • V1 pays for V2
Reason three

Nobody expects perfect

Your buyer wants the one win you promised. Deliver that and they're happy. They are not sitting there grading your design.

  • They want the result
  • It's not a beauty contest
  • Helpful is the bar
Focused, not lazy

Small means focused, not lazy.

There's a real difference between a lean product and a sloppy one, and it matters. Keep this straight.

The line

Lean is not sloppy

A two-page PDF you slapped together in five minutes breaks trust. Small isn't an excuse to cut corners. It's permission to go deep on one thing.

  • Small is not the same as careless
  • One thing, done really well
  • Care always shows
The standard

One problem, solved completely

Solve your one problem so well that someone would happily pay again. That's the bar. Narrow and excellent beats broad and thin every time.

  • Go deep, not wide
  • Leave them genuinely helped
  • Quality on a small scope
“

A great taco truck doesn't have a ten-page menu. It does three things so well there's a line out the door. Your first product is the taco truck. Small menu, no apologies, really good at the one thing.

The finish line

How to know it's actually done.

A simple three-question test so you stop polishing and hit publish.

Question one

Does it deliver the win?

If someone follows it, do they get the one outcome you promised? If yes, it works. That's the only thing that truly has to be true.

Question two

Is it clean and clear?

Readable, consistent, no obvious mistakes. Not gallery-perfect. Just clear enough that nothing trips the reader up.

Question three

Would you give it to a friend?

If you'd hand it to a friend with the same problem and feel good about it, it's ready to sell. Trust that gut.

If it delivers the win, it's ready. Ship it.

Module 2 complete

Call it done. Out loud.

Run your product through the three questions. If it delivers the win, it's clean, and you'd give it to a friend, then it's finished. Say it: this is done. That's Module 2 complete. You've made a real product. Next, we set it up so people can actually pay you for it.

Price It for the First Yes

Module 4 · Lesson 1
Make yes easy.
The Goal of Price Pick a Number Not Underselling Your Move
Core idea

Your first price has one job: make yes easy.

People agonize over pricing like it's a math problem with a right answer. For your first product, it isn't. The goal isn't the most money per sale. It's the most people saying yes for the very first time. Let's pick a number that does exactly that.

The goal of price

Price for the first yes, not the big payday.

Once you know what the price is actually for, the number gets a whole lot easier to pick.

Reason one

The first yes is the prize

Your first product isn't where you get rich. It's where a stranger becomes a buyer. A low price removes the last reason to hesitate.

  • Turn a watcher into a buyer
  • Low price, low risk
  • The relationship is the win
Reason two

Cheap gets tested

When nobody knows you yet, a low price lets people try you without much thought. A high price makes them stop and wonder if you're worth it.

  • Easy to say yes to
  • Skips the "is she legit?" pause
  • More buyers, faster
Reason three

First sales build proof

A handful of happy buyers gives you reviews, confidence, and momentum. That proof is worth more than the few extra dollars a high price might earn.

  • Reviews and trust
  • Confidence to keep going
  • Proof beats price early on

$17 to $47. Pick a number in there and move on.

Where to land

The zone that works

Somewhere between seventeen and forty-seven dollars. Low enough to be an impulse yes, high enough that people take it seriously. Free can feel worthless. This feels real but easy.

  • $17 to $47 is the zone
  • Cheap enough to not overthink
  • Real enough to be valued
A relief

Don't overthink it

There's no perfect number, and you can change it anytime. Pick one that feels a touch low to you. That little discomfort usually means it's priced right for a first product.

  • No magic number exists
  • Change it whenever you want
  • A little low is just right
Not underselling

You're not underselling yourself.

This is the worry that makes people price too high and then sell nothing. Let's settle it for good.

The reframe

It's a doorway, not a ceiling

A low first price isn't you saying you're cheap. It's the easy front door. Once people are in and you've helped them, they'll happily pay more for what comes next.

  • The first price opens the door
  • Bigger offers come later
  • Low now, not low forever
On purpose

You're lowering the bar deliberately

You're making that first yes tiny on purpose, because the first yes is the hard one. Every sale after it gets easier, and it can cost more.

  • First yes: make it easy
  • Later yeses: charge more
  • The ladder goes up from here
“

Think of a coffee shop's first punch on a loyalty card. They're not getting rich on your first latte. They want you walking back in. Your low first price is that first punch on the card.

Land somewhere between seventeen and forty-seven dollars, choose the one that feels a touch low, and lock it in. Don't spend another day on it. Next lesson, we build the simple page that does the selling for you.

Your Sales Page

Module 4 · Lesson 2
Clear sells.
What It Needs Keep It Plain Common Traps Your Move
Core idea

A sales page is just a clear answer to one question.

The phrase "sales page" makes people picture some slick, manipulative thing they don't know how to write. Forget that. A good sales page just answers one honest question: should I buy this? Give a clear yes, and you're done. Here's all it really needs.

What it needs

The few things a sales page actually needs.

Not a wall of copy. Just enough, top to bottom, to help one person decide. Here are the pieces, in order.

★
The one question

Your page answers: should I buy this?

Every piece below helps the right person get to a clear yes.

1

A clear headline

The one outcome they get. Say it plainly, right at the top.

2

The problem it solves

Name their struggle so they feel understood and keep reading.

3

What's inside

A short bulleted list of what they actually get. No mystery.

4

Who it's for

One line so the right person thinks, "that's me."

5

The price and the buy button

Clear and easy to find. Never make them hunt for how to pay.

6

A line about you

One sentence on why you can help. That's all the credibility you need.

Keep it plain

Plain and clear beats clever.

The fancier you try to make it, the more you get in your own way. Three simple rules.

Rule one

Write like you talk

Say it the way you'd say it to a friend across the table. No marketing-speak, no hype words. Your normal voice is the most convincing thing you've got.

  • Talk, don't "sell"
  • Skip the hype words
  • Sound like a person
Rule two

One promise, repeated

Pick the one outcome and keep coming back to it. A page that promises one clear thing beats a page that lists fifteen.

  • One outcome, front and center
  • Repeat it, don't pile on
  • Clarity over cleverness
Rule three

Make the next step obvious

One buy button, easy to find, showing up more than once down the page. Never make someone hunt for how to pay you.

  • One clear button
  • Repeat it down the page
  • No guessing required
Common traps

The traps that sink a simple page.

Avoid these three and your plain little page will outperform fancier ones.

Trap one

Too many words

Long, dense pages bury the decision. People skim. Short and scannable, with room to breathe, wins.

  • Cut, then cut again
  • Short paragraphs
  • White space helps
Trap two

Too many choices

One product, one button, one decision. The second you add options, people freeze and leave without buying anything.

  • One offer only
  • One call to action
  • Don't make them choose
Trap three

Hiding the price

Burying the price feels safer but makes people suspicious. Show it plainly, right by the button. Clear pricing builds trust.

  • Show the price
  • Put it by the button
  • Honesty sells
“

A clear, plain page from a real person beats a slick, clever one every time. Your audience over 40 has seen every sales trick there is. Honest and simple is what earns their click.

Before lesson 3

Draft your page in one sitting.

Headline, the problem, what's inside, who it's for, the price, the button, and a line about you. Write it like you're talking to one person, and keep it short. Then the last lesson is the one people fear most and need most: actually telling people it exists.

Tell People

Module 4 · Lesson 3
Help out loud.
Reframe It The Three Spots One Ask at a Time Your Move
Core idea

You're not selling. You're helping out loud.

Here's the wall almost everyone hits. They build the whole thing and then never tell a soul it exists, because telling people feels pushy. So let's fix the feeling first, then I'll show you exactly where to mention it without ever sounding salesy.

Reframe it

Selling is just helping, out loud.

If the feeling is the problem, change the feeling. Here's what's actually happening when you mention your product.

Truth one

You made it to help

You built this to solve a real problem for a real person. Telling them it exists isn't pushy. Hiding it from someone who needs it is the unkind thing.

  • You're offering a solution
  • Staying quiet doesn't help them
  • Mentioning it is generous
Truth two

It's the friend test

If a friend asked how to fix the exact thing you're good at, you'd just tell them. You wouldn't whisper or apologize. This is that, only out loud on a video.

  • You'd tell a friend
  • No apology needed
  • Same energy, bigger room
Truth three

They can always say no

A soft mention isn't pressure. You're opening a door, not shoving anyone through it. The people who need it will be glad you spoke up.

  • Offer, don't pressure
  • A no is perfectly fine
  • The right people say yes
The three spots

Three places to mention it.

You don't need to be loud or constant. Just present in the right three spots.

Spot one

A soft mention in videos

Around the one-third mark, after you've given value, one calm line: "If you want help with this, I made something, link's in the description." Then right back to the content.

  • One line, then move on
  • Only after you've helped first
  • Point to the description
Spot two

The freebie thank-you page

The page right after someone grabs your freebie. They just raised their hand, so it's the perfect place for a gentle "here's the next step if you want it."

  • They're already warm
  • A natural next step
  • Soft, not a hard pitch
Spot three

One email

A single short email to your list that tells the story of who it helps and points to the page. Not a campaign. One honest note from you.

  • One email, not ten
  • Tell them who it helps
  • Link it and let it breathe
One ask at a time

One spoken ask per video.

This is the rule that keeps you from sounding salesy and keeps your videos working.

The rule

Don't stack your asks

The second you ask for two things out loud, join my community and buy my product, you split attention and get less of both. Pick one spoken ask per video.

  • One ask, said once
  • Don't pile them up
  • The description holds the rest
The trick

Match the ask to the warmth

A cold video gets the soft ask, the freebie. A video about the exact thing your product solves can name the product directly. Warmer viewer, more direct ask.

  • Cold video, soft ask
  • Warm video, name it
  • Let the topic decide
“

Most first products don't fail because they're bad. They fail because nobody ever heard about them. The difference is usually just someone willing to say, out loud, this exists and it can help you.

Module 4 complete

Say it out loud. Just once.

Pick your warmest video, add one soft mention at the one-third mark, set up the thank-you page line, and write the one email. That's the whole selling system, and that's Module 4 done. You've now got a product made, set up to take money, and a way to tell people. The hard part is behind you.
